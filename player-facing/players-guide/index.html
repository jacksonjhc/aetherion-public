    Aetherion — Player's Guide¶

    Welcome to Aetherion, a homebrew Pathfinder 2e campaign. You'll be playing levels 1 through 20 across (we expect) a multi-year run, Saturdays 6-10pm. This guide is for you to read before the campaign starts so you can build a character that fits.

    For the in-world primer — what every Skyborn knows about the continent, the Pantheon, the Pit, and the nations — see The World You Know. This guide focuses on this campaign specifically: the shape of the story, how you're connected, and what kinds of characters will thrive.

    Throughout this guide, mechanical references (classes, feats, skills, etc.) link to the Archives of Nethys for quick reference. Homebrew options link to our own design docs.

    What this campaign is¶

    A 1-20 epic in the home setting your GM has been building. Some shape notes:

    • Anthology at the start; cohesive at the end. Early sessions are mostly self-contained jobs — recover a thing, escort a person, investigate a body. By the middle of the campaign, threads will start braiding into something larger. By the end, you'll be on the kind of quest that L1 characters would have been laughed at for accepting.
    • Stakes that grow with you. You won't start L1 trying to save the world. You'll be on that kind of quest by L20, but you'll have earned it.
    • Plot you can see. Your GM has a story he wants to tell. He's not going to railroad you, but the tracks are there and visible. Take hooks; follow plot threads. If you want to go somewhere unexpected, say so — the GM will pivot.
    • Mystery as content, not as gameplay. You don't have to solve a puzzle to advance the story. The world reveals itself as you play.

    This is not a Kingmaker-style sandbox or a kingdom-building game. There will be some sandbox stretches, but the overall shape is "growing into a story" rather than "building a place."

    What this campaign is about¶

    Themes you'll encounter, in case they help you pick a character:

    • Working for someone whose motives aren't entirely clear, and choosing to trust them anyway
    • The gap between official histories and what the records actually say
    • Communities of outsiders carving out lives in a world that wasn't built for them
    • The cost of mercy and the cost of cruelty
    • Becoming powerful enough to matter to gods

    If you're drawn to characters who chafe against authority, who ask questions, or who quietly take care of people the world has forgotten — there's room for you here. If you'd rather play a true believer who slowly has to reckon with what they were taught — there's room for you too.

    Where the campaign starts¶

    You all live or work in Aurion, capital of Highcrown — the political and religious center of Aetherion. The Skyward Inquisition headquarters here; so does the Majestal Court. It's a city of marble, smoke, scripture, and the loud whisper of orthodoxy.

    You've each ended up taking work from a quirky shopkeeper named Yverin, who calls himself the Antiquarian. He runs a mid-district shop full of old relics, older books, and what most people would call useless dust. He pays well for small jobs — recoveries, deliveries, the occasional discreet errand. He's widely considered eccentric but harmless. He's old. He's wry. He talks too much about ancient history and not enough about himself.

    You don't ask too many questions. The pay is good.

    Why you're together¶

    The group's agreed framing: you work for the same private patron — Yverin. Beyond that, you decide your relationships. Were you hired together? Hired separately and met on the job? Old friends who answered the same ad? Strangers who learned to be useful to each other? Up to you.

    Some questions to think about for your character:

    • How did Yverin find you, or how did you find him?
    • What did he offer that you needed?
    • Why do you keep coming back?

    Character creation¶

    Basics¶

    • Starting level: 1
    • Ancestry: any PF2e ancestry your GM approves (see notes below)
    • Class: any PF2e class (see notes below)
    • Background: standard PF2e backgrounds work; campaign-specific backgrounds are in development (see below)
    • Ability scores: standard PF2e creation
    • Hit points & equipment: standard

    Ancestries — what's common¶

    The five Skyborn ancestries dominate Aetherion society. Each has a "home" nation and a cultural reputation, though plenty of people live outside their home:

    • Human (AoN) — Highcrown majority; politically and religiously dominant; the "default" ancestry of the continent's power structures.
    • Elf (AoN) — Sylvaris majority; scholarly, long-lived, somewhat insular; lots of contact with the Archivists of Moonbough.
    • Dwarf (AoN) — Ankarum majority; fortress culture, formal, structural engineering; the Keelwrights literally hold the continent together.
    • Halfling (AoN) — Tumblebrook majority; agricultural, communal, gentle; feeds the rest of Aetherion.
    • Gnome (AoN) — Glymswick majority; inventive, irreverent, mechanical; the Gearwright Consortium runs Crowngear and the drift control that keeps Aetherion mobile.

    Ancestries — versatile heritages¶

    Half-elves, half-orcs, and tieflings all exist in Aetherion. They're more common than polite society admits. The Skyward Inquisition gets twitchy about visible "hybrids" but rarely acts on it openly in cosmopolitan Aurion. If you want to play a character with an unusual heritage — a striking visual feature, a partial lineage, something the world doesn't have a name for — talk to the GM; we'll find a way to make it work.

    Ancestries — uncommon and rare¶

    • Awakened Animal: Exists, but extremely rare in Aetherion. The Inquisition treats awakened animals with suspicion. If you want to play one, talk to the GM — there's a campaign-specific path for this concept that gives your character an interesting place in the world.
    • Other Lost Omens ancestries: Generally available with GM approval. Some will fit Aetherion cleanly; some will need a conversation.

    Classes — what fits where¶

    Aetherion is friendly to almost every PF2e class. Some notes that might shape your concept:

    Divine and faith-based - Cleric / Champion of the Twelve — pillars of Skyborn society. Public, respected, and watched by the Inquisition. Lots of mechanical and narrative support. - Cleric / Champion of an unsanctioned power — technically possible, deeply unwise. Don't pick this without a long conversation. - Oracle — exists but draws attention. Strange visions are often diagnosed as illness or possession by orthodox priests.

    Arcane - Wizard / Magus — the Sylvari academies are the seat of formal arcane study, but practitioners exist across all five nations. - Witch — exists; eccentric patrons raise eyebrows. - Sorcerer — common; bloodline backgrounds work as normal.

    Primal and occult - Druid — exists, but Skyborn druidic traditions favor sky and storm and wind over deep-forest nature. - Bard — beloved everywhere; the festival circuits in Tumblebrook and the court masques in Aurion both want them. - Psychic — uncommon, mystical, draws attention from the Inquisition.

    Martial - Fighter / Barbarian / Ranger — common in all nations. - Rogue / Investigator — Aurion has more shadow than light. Plenty of room. - Monk — uncommon, considered exotic; some Sylvari traditions support it. - Swashbuckler / Gunslinger — both fit; gunslingers especially work for Glymswick characters or Cogbarrow-trained ones.

    Newer classes - Kineticist — rare but accepted. An esoteric tradition. Aetherion mythologizes air, water, metal, and wood more than earth and fire, but all elements exist. - Alchemist — common in Glymswick; the Crowngear Consortium loves an alchemist. - Magus / Thaumaturge / Inventor / Summoner — all available; expect some side-eye from the orthodoxy depending on flavor. - Commander — Khalvar Hold's strategists; Ankarum produces a lot of these. - Guardian — common across the five nations; "stand in front, take the hit" is a respected role.

    If a class isn't on this list, it's still probably available — ask.

    Backgrounds¶

    All standard PF2e backgrounds work. We've also written a slate of campaign-specific backgrounds — see the full list with mechanical details at Backgrounds (Campaign-Specific).

    Picks include shop work for Yverin, Highcrown institutions (Inquisition, Crown Assembly, Skyguard), Sylvari scholarship, Ankarum dwarven craft, Glymswick engineering, Tumblebrook pastoral, Tether's End liminal, religious vocations, outsider/wanderer, and awakened-animal-friendly options. Some are tagged "Talk to the GM" — they have heretical or politically sensitive ties and just need a quick chat before character creation.

    If none fit your concept, talk to the GM — backgrounds are easy to bend or build from scratch.

    Skills¶

    Skills that pay off across the campaign:

    • Society — political, factional, and historical knowledge. Will be useful constantly.
    • Religion — public theology of the Twelve, the calendar, the iconography.
    • Arcana / Occultism — shards and weird relics show up a lot.
    • Stealth / Thievery / Deception — Yverin's work is not always above-board.
    • Diplomacy — for talking to fences, nobles, scholars, and the occasional Inquisitor.

    Useful but not central:

    • Athletics — always pays off in combat-heavy sessions.
    • Survival — most of the campaign starts urban but the wilderness pays off later.
    • Nature — strong for druids/rangers; less central than Religion/Society for most.
    • Performance — surprisingly useful for an Aurion-set early game.

    Lore skills worth picking up¶

    If your background gives you a lore slot or you want to grab one with a skill increase:

    • Aurion Lore — the home city; streets, factions, neighborhoods.
    • Inquisition Lore — how they operate, who their senior figures are.
    • Skyfall Lore — the falling-relic phenomenon and its folklore.
    • Crowngear Lore — Glymswick technology, drift control, mechanical artifice.
    • Sky Travel Lore — bridges, ropeways, caravans, airships.

    Useful feats to consider¶

    This is suggestion, not prescription. Feats that tend to pay off in Aetherion's early sessions:

    • Streetwise / Connections / Group Impression — for navigating Aurion's social fabric.
    • Pickpocket / Subtle Theft — Yverin's errands often shade gray.
    • Recognize Spell / Trick Magic Item — shards and relics are everywhere.
    • Quick Identification / Assurance (Religion or Society) — knowing what's in front of you fast.
    • Diehard / Toughness — Aurion has more violence than its facade suggests.

    Common, uncommon, and rare options¶

    Pathfinder 2e flags some feats, spells, items, ancestries, and class options as uncommon or rare — meaning you can't just pick them up the same way as common options. In Aetherion, treat that flag as an in-world question: where did your character learn this thing, and is the world ready to admit they know it?

    • Common options are anyone's. Pick freely.
    • Uncommon options need GM approval plus a one-sentence in-fiction reason — a teacher, a relic you found, a tradition you were raised in, a lineage that explains the trait. Most uncommon picks will be cleared cheerfully; the conversation is mostly to weave them into your backstory.
    • Rare options are story-locked. Don't expect to take one at character creation. Expect to earn the opportunity in play. If you have a strong concept that lives at the rare tier, talk to the GM early.

    The same logic applies to gear, alchemical formulas, weapon traits, and most class options that aren't flagged common. The answer is usually yes — but the question matters.

    Shards (campaign-specific mechanic)¶

    You're going to find shards. A lot of them, eventually. A few things to know:

    • Shards are bits of crystallized... something. Old, valuable, weird. They sometimes whisper. They sometimes show wielders flashes of memory that aren't their own.
    • Mechanically: PF2e relics. If you've used relic rules before, that's the framework.
    • No corruption track. Bonding with a shard won't make your character evil or possessed. The pressure is purely narrative.
    • You'll likely bond with one over the campaign. Voluntary. Reversible until very late.
    • More detail at first session. For now: don't avoid shards out of habit, and don't grab them as expected loot either. They have weight.

    Table expectations¶

    What we ask of you:

    • One character for the campaign. If your character dies, we build a new one together. (Not the focus of this campaign, though — character death will be rare in the published-AP sense.)
    • Show up if you can. We meet weekly. Missing a session is fine; the story is built to tolerate it, and you won't lose your seat or critical story moments.
    • Engage with the world. You don't have to be a master roleplayer. You don't have to do voices. But talk to named NPCs, follow plot hooks you can see, and let the story land.
    • Tell us what you want. If a thread isn't working for you, say so. If you want more focus on something — a class feature, a relationship, a backstory thread — ask. The GM is happy to pivot.
    • Bring a backstory. Even one paragraph is enough. Give the GM something to grab. The earlier you can share it, the more of it can show up in play.

    Pacing¶

    • Saturday 6-10pm, weekly. Same slot as Kingmaker.
    • This campaign starts after Kingmaker concludes. No firm date yet; track the Nyrissa arc.
    • Roughly 1 level per 4-6 sessions, varying by adventure shape.
    • Multi-year run. Plan accordingly. Build a character you'll still want to play in two years.

    Backstory prompts¶

    Use as much or as little as helps. Even just a few sentences for each is great.

    1. How did Yverin find you, or how did you find him?
    2. What does he pay you for? What kind of work do you do for him?
    3. What's your relationship to Aurion? Native? Recent arrival? Returning after years away?
    4. What's your relationship to the Twelve? Devout? Lapsed? Cynical? Fearful? Curious?
    5. What's your relationship to the Inquisition? Awe? Distrust? Personal grudge? Indifference?
    6. What do you want? Money? Knowledge? Family? Vengeance? To be left alone? To matter to someone?
    7. What's something other characters would notice about you on first meeting? A scar, a tic, a turn of phrase, an unusual possession.

    You don't need to answer all of these. Pick what helps. The GM will work with whatever you give him.

    Open questions / what's still in flux¶

    This guide is a working draft — it'll evolve as we approach session one. Things still being firmed up:

    • The home nation. Aurion / Highcrown is the working assumption (it's where Yverin's shop is). Talk to the GM if you wanted to build a character from elsewhere with a strong reason to be in Aurion.
    • Final mechanical drafts of the campaign-specific backgrounds. Coming before session 1.
    • Awakened-animal path. Talk to the GM if this concept calls to you.
    • Any rule we should house-rule. If there's a PF2e rule you've found wonky, raise it now.

    Asking questions¶

    Ask the GM anytime. There are no dumb questions — especially during character creation. The sooner we hear what you're building, the more we can integrate your character into the campaign's opening.
