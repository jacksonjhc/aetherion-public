    House Rules: Magic Items & Gear¶

    A few things work differently in this campaign from a by-the-book Pathfinder game. They all serve the same goal — making the gear you find actually matter — and they're meant to be read as one package. There are four; each makes the others click.

    The short version: the boring math every character needs to keep up (the +1/+2/+3 on your attacks, AC, and saves) is now free and automatic. In exchange, nobody crafts permanent magic items, and the interesting gear you get comes from what you find in the world rather than from a catalogue of every item in every book.

    Why we're doing this. In a normal game, the smart-money move is to take the crafting feats, melt down everything you find, and build the mathematically perfect kit out of the rulebooks. It's a fair thing to want — but it makes found treasure pointless (you'd just break it down anyway), and it kicks off an arms race: the stronger your gear gets, the stronger I make the monsters, and we end up exactly where we started except every fight takes longer. These rules take the math off the table so the gear you find can be the fun part again. In this world, powerful items are recovered things — old relics, shard-gear, pieces with a history — not something you stamp out at a workbench.

    House Rule 1 — No crafting of permanent magic items¶

    You cannot craft permanent magic items (weapons, armor, runes, worn or held wondrous items). The skill feat that normally enables this — Magical Crafting — won't produce permanent magical gear in this campaign.

    What you CAN still do:

    • Use the Crafting skill normally — Repair, Earn Income, identify and reverse-engineer formulas, craft non-magical gear.
    • Craft consumables. Potions, scrolls, oils, talismans, and alchemical items (bombs, elixirs, mutagens) are all fair game. Make as many as you like.
    • Play an Alchemist at full power. The Alchemist's daily item creation (Advanced Alchemy) and Alchemical Crafting are completely untouched. Nothing here weakens that class.
    • Sell unwanted gear for gold, and buy what's available. The economy still runs (see House Rule 3) — you just can't manufacture permanent magic items yourself.

    So the maker fantasy is still open to you through consumables and alchemy. What's gone is "I'll just build the perfect permanent loadout from the books."

    This applies to NPCs too. No one in the world mass-produces or custom-builds permanent magic items — there's no artificer in the capital who'll make you a thing to order. (Attaching or moving a rune you already have isn't "making" anything — that service still exists; see House Rule 3.)

    House Rule 2 — Automatic Bonus Progression (ABP)¶

    We're using the official Automatic Bonus Progression variant rule. 📖 Read it on Archives of Nethys: https://2e.aonprd.com/Rules.aspx?ID=2741

    What it means in plain terms¶

    Normally, every PF2e character is quietly required to keep buying bigger numbers — a +1 weapon, then +2, then +3; striking runes for more damage; resilient runes for your saves; an apex item for your key ability. It isn't really a choice; it's a tax everyone pays to keep pace with the monster math, and falling behind it hurts.

    ABP deletes the treadmill. Those bonuses are granted to you automatically as you level up — the same for everyone, no item required, no shopping. You never chase them again. That frees every magic item you do find to be something interesting instead of a mandatory math-stick.

    What you gain automatically, by level¶

    Level You automatically gain
    2 Attack potency +1
    3 Skill potency (one skill +1)
    4 Devastating Attacks (striking — +1 weapon damage die)
    5 Defense potency +1 (AC)
    6 Skill potency (a 2nd skill +1)
    7 Perception potency +1
    8 Saving-throw potency +1
    9 Skill potency (one →+2, add another +1)
    10 Attack potency +2
    11 Defense potency +2
    12 Devastating Attacks (greater striking — +2 dice)
    13 Perception +2, skill adjustments
    14 Saving-throw potency +2
    15 Skill potency (a 3rd →+2, 4th +1)
    16 Attack potency +3
    17 Attribute apex (+1 to a key ability, or to +4)
    18 Defense potency +3
    19 Devastating Attacks (major striking — +3 dice), Perception +3
    20 Saving-throw potency +3, final skill bumps

    What this replaces — don't go looking for these¶

    You'll no longer find, buy, need, or miss any of the following — they effectively don't exist for us:

    • Weapon potency & striking runes (your attack and damage) — automatic.
    • Armor potency & resilient runes (your AC and saves) — automatic.
    • Apex items (the big ability-score items) — replaced by the level-17 attribute apex.
    • Flat "+X item bonus to a skill / Perception / save" items — automatic.

    What this does NOT touch — this is the loot that matters now¶

    Everything interesting about magic items is still completely in play, and is now the whole point of finding gear:

    • Property runes — flaming, frost, ghost touch, returning, grievous, all of them. They still go on your weapons and armor. (Under ABP, the number of property runes you can carry scales with your automatic potency, so you're never short-changed.)
    • Wondrous, worn, and held items with abilities — boots that let you leap, a cloak that casts a spell, a horn that does a thing. Items that do something rather than just add a number.
    • Consumables — potions, scrolls, wands, talismans, alchemy.
    • Relics and shards — the campaign's special, story-bearing gear. These grow with you. (Mechanically, PF2e relics; more on these at session one.)

    The upshot: every magic item you find is now a real, exciting choice — because it isn't competing with a +2 striking rune you're saving up to buy.

    Playing a Kineticist? You're covered.¶

    You might remember the Gate Attenuator — the worn item that boosts your impulse attacks. You won't need to hunt one down. Our rule:

    ABP attack potency applies to all attack rolls — weapon Strikes, spell attacks, and impulse attack rolls alike.

    So your Elemental Blast and other impulse attacks pick up the automatic bonus exactly like a fighter's sword — and it climbs to +3, where the Gate Attenuator capped at +2, so you come out slightly ahead. Your save-based impulse DCs were never item-boosted anyway, and your impulse damage scales with your class, so nothing there changes. The Constitution bump the top-tier Gate Attenuator used to give is covered by ABP's level-17 attribute apex.

    📖 Gate Attenuator, for reference: https://2e.aonprd.com/Equipment.aspx?ID=2654

    (The "spell attacks count" half of that rule means any caster who makes spell attack rolls is covered too.)

    House Rule 3 — Gear comes from the world, not the catalogue¶

    Permanent magic items aren't a catalogue you order from. They're things you find, are given, recover, or track down in the world. There's still a working economy:

    • You can sell gear you don't want for gold (standard rates).
    • You can buy what's in stock. I'll keep you a running list of what local sources, contacts, and markets actually have on hand — a real shop's real inventory, not "anything printed in any book." What the shop has is what the shop has. You can't special-order a permanent magic item a vendor doesn't already stock.
    • The crafting ban applies to NPCs too. No blacksmith, artificer, or back-room "magic shop" will custom-make permanent magic gear to your spec — they can't, any more than you can. (Mundane gear is fine: a smith will happily forge, repair, or special-order ordinary weapons, armor, and tools.)
    • You can still pursue a specific item — by finding it, not ordering it. If you want a particular piece, the path is a lead, a favour, a quest, or an expedition to recover one that already exists somewhere in the world. That's discovery, not manufacture.
    • Property runes: loose or attached, both fine. Buy a loose property rune when one's on the available list, or find one already etched onto gear. And while nobody makes permanent magic, an NPC runesmith can attach or relocate a rune you've legitimately acquired (the standard PF2e Transfer Runes activity) for a fee. Moving existing magic around is allowed; conjuring new magic from raw materials isn't.

    Because the mandatory math is handled by ABP, the available list can be short and characterful without leaving anyone underpowered. The gear you get will be chosen and placed — it'll mean something.

    House Rule 4 — The world adapts (no permanent auto-win combos)¶

    Bring clever plans — I want that, and a smart setup absolutely should win you a fight. What won't work is a combo that deletes an entire category of encounter and then keeps deleting it forever.

    A classic example: everyone gets darkvision, the party caster drops magical darkness on every fight, and suddenly every sighted enemy is blinded while you see fine. The first time you pull that off, it's great play — enjoy it. But it won't stay a free win, for two reasons:

    • Gear is found, not mass-produced (House Rules 1–3). You usually can't kit the whole party with the same enabling item, because a universal exploit almost always needs everyone to have the trick — and how many of a thing exist is up to the world.
    • The world is varied, and its inhabitants aren't stupid. Not every threat has the weakness your combo targets — plenty of things don't rely on sight at all, some bring their own light or see straight through magical darkness, and intelligent enemies who survive an encounter learn from it. The trick stays a useful tool; it never becomes an off-switch.

    This isn't a promise to punish creativity — it's the opposite. A combo that auto-wins everything actually makes the game boring, including for you, and it kicks off the exact arms race ABP exists to end. So instead, the world answers cleverness with cleverness — and your good ideas stay good ideas instead of becoming the only idea.

    What this means for your character (quick FAQ)¶

    • "I wanted to be the party crafter." Still on the table: consumables, scrolls, potions, alchemy, repairs, non-magical gear. The Alchemist class is 100% intact. You just don't manufacture permanent magic items.
    • "Do I still get magic items?" Yes — the interesting ones, more reliably, and they'll suit you. You just stop chasing the boring +X math, because you get it free.
    • "Am I weaker than a normal PF2e character?" No. ABP hands you the same attack/AC/save/damage curve a fully-kitted character would grind toward — automatically, on schedule. You simply can't exceed the intended curve by min-maxing the item economy, which is the whole point.
    • "Can I just order the exact item I want?" No special orders for permanent magic gear, and no NPC will custom-craft one — what a shop has in stock is what's available. You find specific items through play. Mundane gear you can absolutely have made or special-ordered.
    • "Can I build a strategy that hard-counters a type of enemy?" For a fight or two — absolutely, that's smart play and I'll reward it. Just don't expect it to trivialise every encounter of that type forever (House Rule 4).
    • "Does my money still matter?" Yes — consumables, property runes and special gear off the available list, services, downtime, and mundane commissions all cost coin. You'll just need less of it, since the expensive math runes are gone.

    On Foundry¶

    We're running on Foundry VTT. ABP is a built-in setting your GM enables once at world setup — your character sheet will apply all of these bonuses automatically as you level, so there's nothing extra for you to track or buy. Just leave fundamental runes off your gear (you don't need them) and add property runes as you find them.

    Questions about any of this are welcome — bring them to session zero. If there's a PF2e rule you've found wonky and think we should house-rule, now's the time to raise it. See also the Player's Guide.
